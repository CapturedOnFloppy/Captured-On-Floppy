All Mavicas allow you to set the time and date, which populate the "date created" metadata and timestamp your photos. Recent FD models (like the FD-92) may lose time when the main battery is removed—due to a backup coin cell battery. Some models use a disposable CR2025 coin cell, others (FD-92, FD87, FD97, FD100, FD200) use a rechargeable MS621FE soldered to the board.

What you'll need:

  • The replacement battery: MS621FE lithium cell
  • Soldering iron, flux, solder
  • Desoldering tool (wick or sucker)
  • #0 or #00 Philips screwdriver

    Quick Steps:
  • Open the camera (remove main battery first, unscrew all outside screws)
  • Disconnect ribbon cables and display/control PCB; locate old battery
  • Desolder and remove the old battery
  • Prep and fit the new MS621FE battery into place
  • Solder new battery, clean up, reassemble camera
  • Take your time reassembling, mind the ribbon cable locks

Here's how to replace it!

Step 1: Open up the Camera.
REMOVE THE MAIN BATTERY FIRST!!!
On the FD-92 and similar models, opening the camera simple. Remove all eight screws around the exterior and pull the two halves apart! Carefully disconnect the ribbon cable holding the two primary boards together.

Remove all eight of the marked screws to open the camera

Disconnect the ribbon cable connecting the two halves.


Step 2: Remove the display/control PCB.
Remove all the screws marked in the image below (blue). Then, lift the metal shield plate away from the board. Finally, disconnect the two remaining ribbon cables (one is hidden below the shield plate) and the speaker connector.
The old battery will be in the location marked by the green arrow, a small coin cell battery.
Remove all screws (blue) and remaining connections (yellow). Old battery will be at green arrow.


Step 3: Remove the old battery.
This can be a bit tricky, especially if you haven't done much solder work. On the back side of the PCB you'll find both connection points for the old battery, labeled + and -. Using your desoldering tools, remove the solder from the battery connections and remove the battery from the board.
Step 4: Prep the new battery.
While the Digikey part number above is a pretty good fit, it's not the exact same item original to the cameras. To make it fit, we'll need to first bend the legs down at a 90 degree angle. Be careful not to connect both legs if you use a metal tool, these batteries don't hold much energy, but a short is still not good for them.
Make sure that you bend both legs AWAY from the positive side of the battery (see 'after' pic below).
Once done, test fit the battery into the holes on the board. You'll probably find that it's a bit tight. Bend the legs away from each other slightly to make sure everything fits and the battery can lay flat against the board.
Remember that the "+" side of the battery should be facing away from the board, and the battery should be on the side of the board WITHOUT the +/- marks. If this is confusing, use the last picture in this post as a reference for the battery's orientation.
New battery ready to be soldered.


Step 5: Solder the new battery.
With the new battery on the board (and double checking the orientation reminder from the previous step) solder both legs of the battery. I recommend using flux and trying not to apply heat for too long, as too much heat might damage the new battery. Clean up any flux and you should be good to go!
New solder joints :)


Step 6: Re-assembly.
With the new battery installed, reverse the disassembly steps to put the camera back together. For the connectors on the display/control board, make sure that the brown lock goes ABOVE the ribbon cable (blue) and the black lock goes BELOW the ribbon cable (green).
Don't forget the speaker connection and the metal shield on the PCB!
Take your time when bringing the two halves of the camera back together, there's a handful of things that need to line up.
Make sure the blue connector's lock goes above the ribbon cable, and the green connector's lock goes below the cable.



New battery installed in top-right!
With everything back together you should be good to go! I haven't tested how long the rechargeable battery keeps time yet, but I know it is at least a few hours. Odds are it should be good for weeks or even months. Sony likely used a similar system to a digital watch.
