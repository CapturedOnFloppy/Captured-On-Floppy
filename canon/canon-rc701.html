Canon RC-701: Full Details

Historical Context

Release Date: 1986
Lineage: The RC-701 was Canon’s first commercial still video camera, and one of the earliest electronic capture cameras ever marketed for professional use. It was a direct competitor to Sony’s Mavica analog lineup, especially in the press and broadcast fields, and helped usher in the era of “electronic still photography.”
Market Position: Targeted at photojournalists, news agencies, and professionals needing instant electronic image capture and transmission, at a time when film was still dominant.

Detailed Features

1. Sensor & Image Quality

  • 2/3" CCD sensor delivers 570 x 490 pixel analog video stills (NTSC), much lower than modern digital but competitive in the era.
  • Image output as composite video signal for instant playback on TV monitors or video printers.

2. Lens & Zoom

  • Accepts all Canon FD-mount SLR lenses, enabling wide choice of prime/zoom glass and creative control.
  • Macro, telephoto, and specialty lenses supported.

3. Storage & Transfer

  • Saves images to 2-inch video floppy disk (VF-50), up to 50 images per disk.
  • Images are stored as analog frames, playable on compatible video equipment.
  • Quick image review and transfer compared to film.

4. Display & Controls

  • Monochrome CRT electronic viewfinder for composition and review.
  • No built-in LCD; external monitor can be connected for group review or studio work.
  • Shutter speed, aperture, and exposure settings via dials and lens controls.

5. Flash & Output

  • Hot shoe and PC sync for external flash use.
  • Composite video output for instant image review or broadcast.

User Experience

  • Image Quality: Revolutionary for the time, but with video-level resolution and analog artifacts; best for newsprint, broadcast, and quick documentation.
  • Lens Flexibility: Full FD-mount compatibility made it uniquely versatile for professionals.
  • Storage: Fast, reusable, and easy to archive compared to film, but each disk held just 50 stills.
  • Controls: SLR-like manual controls familiar to film photographers, but with instant playback.
  • Portability: Large and heavy by today’s standards, but much faster than film for breaking news and events.

Legacy & Impact

  • Pioneer in Electronic Imaging: The RC-701 was a landmark in the shift from film to electronic still capture, used at major events like the 1987 World Athletics Championships and 1988 Seoul Olympics.
  • Professional Adoption: Became a staple among agencies needing fast image turnaround for print and TV.
  • Collector Value: Highly prized by camera historians and collectors as one of the first true still video cameras.

Comparison to Other Cameras

  • Sony Mavica (Analog): Direct competitor, similar video floppy storage, but Canon offered SLR lens compatibility.
  • Canon RC-760: Successor with improved features and ergonomics.
  • Modern Digital Cameras: Far higher resolution, digital storage, but the workflow and instant review pioneered by the RC-701 paved the way.

Trivia & Notes

  • The RC-701 package included a dedicated video floppy recorder (VR-701) for playback and image management.
  • Images could be transmitted by phone line or broadcast via TV, a huge leap over film-based workflows.
  • Used at the 1988 Seoul Olympics, the RC-701 helped establish electronic still imaging in global newsrooms.

My Opinion:


The Canon RC-701 stands as a landmark in the history of photography—a bold and ambitious first step into the world of electronic imaging. Released in 1986, the RC-701 was not just Canon’s first still video camera, but also one of the earliest attempts by any manufacturer to free photographers from the constraints of film. It arrived at a time when newsrooms and agencies were desperate for speed, and the ability to instantly record and transmit images—albeit at analog video resolution—was revolutionary.

What truly set the RC-701 apart was its professional focus and adaptability. Unlike many early electronic cameras that used fixed lenses, the RC-701 accepted Canon’s FD-mount SLR lenses, enabling photographers to use familiar glass for everything from wide-angle news shots to telephoto sports photography. This gave photojournalists a creative edge and ensured the camera could be seamlessly integrated into existing workflows. The electronic viewfinder and instant playback—features we now take for granted—were game-changers for professionals who needed to review and deliver images on the spot.

Where the RC-701 excelled was in bridging the worlds of analog and digital. While it still recorded analog video frames to 2-inch video floppy disks, its workflow was a clear precursor to modern digital photography: shoot, review, transmit. The RC-701 made its mark on major global events like the 1988 Seoul Olympics, proving itself as a reliable tool for rapid, on-the-go reporting. Its quick-turnaround capabilities transformed photojournalism and set the stage for the digital revolution that followed.

In hindsight, the RC-701’s image quality and bulk may seem quaint, but its legacy is profound. It’s celebrated by collectors and camera historians as a pioneering milestone—a camera that didn’t just adapt to a changing world, but helped create it. The RC-701 reminds us that every digital shutter click today owes a debt to the brave, analog steps taken by innovators like Canon nearly four decades ago. I have not owned one but found them technically interesting and is why RC-701 made the website picks.
