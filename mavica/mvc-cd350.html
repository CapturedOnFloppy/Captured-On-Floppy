Sony Mavica MVC-CD350: Full Details

Historical Context

Release Date: 2003
Lineage: The CD350 was a mainstream model in the later era of the CD Mavica series, positioned between the entry-level CD250 and the advanced CD400. It offered a balance of resolution, ease-of-use, and the unique advantage of direct-to-CD storage.
Market Position: Targeted at families, enthusiasts, and small businesses who wanted high-resolution images and the convenience of instant archiving and sharing, without the complexity or price tag of the pro-oriented CD400.

Detailed Features

1. Sensor & Image Quality

  • 3.2MP resolution: Delivers crisp images suitable for prints up to 8x10".
  • JPEG mode for efficient storage and easy sharing.

2. Lens & Zoom

  • 3x optical zoom covers most everyday scenarios, from group shots to moderate telephoto.
  • Decent f/2.8–3.0 maximum aperture for indoor and outdoor shooting.
  • Macro mode for close-up photography.

3. Storage & Transfer

  • 156MB per mini CD, allowing for over 100 high-quality photos per disc.
  • USB port for fast downloads to PC.
  • JPEG and MPEG recording.

4. Display & Controls

  • Large 2.5" LCD and electronic viewfinder for flexible composition.
  • Simple menu system suitable for non-experts.
  • Basic manual controls for exposure and white balance.

5. Flash & Output

  • Built-in pop-up flash for indoor and low-light shooting.

User Experience

  • Image Quality: A clear upgrade from floppy-based Mavicas, making it a solid choice for general photography and snapshots.
  • Storage: Direct-to-CD archiving was ideal for backing up, sharing, or organizing large batches of photos without extra accessories.
  • Controls: Straightforward interface made it easy for families and casual users to operate, while still offering some manual tweaks.
  • Portability: Sturdy and slightly bulky, but manageable for travel and events.

Legacy & Impact

  • Mainstream CD Mavica: Brought the convenience and novelty of CD storage to a wider audience, at a more accessible price point.
  • Collector Value: Appreciated by those who want a practical, reliable, and nostalgic digital camera experience.

Comparison to Other Mavica Models

  • CD500: 5MP, advanced model with more manual controls and better image quality.
  • CD400: 4MP, pro-level, features a Zeiss lens and hot shoe.
  • CD250: 2MP, more entry-level, fewer features.
  • CD1000: 2.1MP, 10x zoom, earlier and bulkier design.

Trivia & Notes

  • The CD350 was among the last Mavicas, combining the classic Mavica workflow with competitive early-2000s specs.
  • No hot shoe or TIFF mode, but offered a great balance of features for most users.
  • CD-RW support allowed users to reuse discs, which was a cost-saving bonus at the time.

My Opinion:


The Sony Mavica CD350 is a fascinating example of Sony’s commitment to practical innovation at a turning point in digital photography. By 2003, the idea of saving photos directly to mini CD-Rs or CD-RWs may have seemed quirky to some, but for many users, it was a revelation: no more worries about limited floppy disk space or the hassle of finding compatible memory cards. With its 3.2-megapixel sensor, the CD350 produced images that were sharp and vibrant enough for everyday prints, web sharing, and documentation—on par with many popular point-and-shoots of its time.

Where the CD350 truly excelled was in its workflow. Being able to shoot over a hundred photos, then simply hand someone a CD that could be loaded into almost any computer, was empowering for families, educators, and small businesses alike. The camera’s large, bright LCD and straightforward controls made it accessible even for beginners, while the inclusion of macro mode and exposure tweaks gave enthusiasts some creative flexibility. USB connectivity meant you weren’t limited to just CD-based transfers, adding another layer of convenience.

The CD350 was not the most advanced or feature-rich Mavica, but it was arguably one of the most user-friendly and practical. It struck a balance between image quality, ease of use, and storage innovation. Today, the CD350 stands as a nostalgic reminder of a time when Sony was willing to experiment and lead with unique solutions—making digital photography more approachable, shareable, and fun for everyone.
