Sony Mavica MVC-CD250: Full Details

Historical Context

  • Release Date: 2002
  • Lineage: Launched alongside the CD200, the CD250 is essentially the same camera with minor body or firmware tweaks, often region-specific.
  • Market Position: Entry-level to mid-range; designed for families, students, and first-time digital buyers.

Detailed Features

1. Sensor & Image Quality

  • 2MP CCD for typical snapshot needs.
  • JPEG only, no TIFF.

2. Lens & Zoom

  • 3x zoom covers most family and travel situations.

3. Storage & Transfer

  • 156MB per CD; more than enough for long trips or events.
  • CD-RW support.

4. Display & Controls

  • LCD-only operation, simple menus.

5. Flash & Output

  • Built-in flash, AV out for TV playback.

User Experience

  • Image Quality: Good for family and web use.
  • Storage: CDs lasted for hundreds of photos—no more disk swapping.
  • Speed: Slightly slow save times due to CD writing.
  • Portability: Compact and easy to handle for a CD camera.

Legacy & Impact

  • Mainstream CD: Brought CD-based digital cameras to the everyday user.
  • Collector Value: Not rare, but prized as a representative of the CD Mavica era.

Comparison to Other Mavica Models

  • CD200: Twin model, nearly identical.
  • CD1000: Larger, older, more zoom.
  • CD350: Newer, with 3.2MP sensor.

Trivia & Notes

  • Sold as a “starter kit” in many regions, bundled with blank CDs and software.
  • No TIFF mode—JPEG only, unlike the CD1000.
  • Often chosen for school or youth group projects due to simplicity.

My Opinion:


The Sony Mavica CD250 marks a pivotal point in the final evolution of the Mavica line, delivering a balanced blend of improved imaging and the hallmark convenience of direct-to-CD storage. With its 2.0-megapixel sensor and 3x optical zoom lens, the CD250 provided solid image quality for everyday use—perfectly suitable for documentation, family moments, and web sharing at a time when such specs were still competitive. Its JPEG compression made for efficient use of the mini CD-Rs, and the camera’s autofocus was reliable, making it approachable for beginners and casual users alike.

Where the CD250 truly excelled was in its user-friendly approach to archiving and sharing. The ability to burn hundreds of photos directly onto a mini CD-R meant users could instantly hand over a disc for viewing on almost any computer—no adapters, card readers, or special software required. This workflow was invaluable for event photographers, educators, and business users who needed rapid, physical backups and effortless distribution. The CD250’s streamlined controls and compact body made it even more accessible than earlier CD Mavicas, bridging the gap between the legacy of floppy-based cameras and the full adoption of optical digital storage.

In summary, the CD250’s strength lay in its simplicity and practicality. It wasn’t the most feature-rich or highest-resolution Mavica, but it brought the unique advantages of the CD system to a wider audience in a camera that was both easy to use and highly dependable. Today, the CD250 is fondly remembered by collectors and enthusiasts as a symbol of Sony’s innovative spirit at the end of the Mavica era—an accessible, forward-thinking device that made digital photography and instant archiving available to everyone.
