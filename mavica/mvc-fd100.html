Sony Mavica MVC-FD100: Full Details

Historical Context

  • Release Date: 2002
  • Lineage: The FD100 was part of the very last generation of Sony’s Mavica line, alongside the FD200, marking the final evolution of floppy-based digital cameras. It was released as a “bridge” model that combined classic floppy disk compatibility with the then-new Memory Stick storage.
  • Market Position: Aimed at advanced consumers, educators, and business users who wanted the convenience of floppy disks but needed higher resolution, video capability, and modern storage flexibility.

Detailed Features

1. Sensor & Image Quality

  • 2.0 Megapixels: 1600 x 1200 resolution, suitable for 8x10" prints and flexible cropping.
  • JPEG Compression: “Fine” and “Standard”; limited images per floppy at highest quality.
  • BMP Option: For uncompressed images (very few per disk).

2. Lens & Zoom

  • 3x Optical Zoom: 41–123mm (35mm equivalent), suitable for everyday photography.
  • Aperture: f/2.8 (wide) to f/3.0 (tele).
  • Macro Mode: Focuses as close as 1cm for detailed close-ups.

3. Storage & Transfer

  • Hybrid Storage: Simultaneous support for floppy disk and internal Memory Stick (no adapter required).
  • Memory Stick: Enables much larger capacities and video recording.
  • Floppy Disk: Universal compatibility with PCs, but slow write and low capacity.

4. Movie Mode

  • MPEG-EX Movie Clips: Only on Memory Stick, not on floppy.
  • Duration: Limited by Memory Stick capacity.

5. Display & Controls

  • LCD: 2.5" color screen for composing and reviewing images.
  • Electronic Viewfinder: Useful in bright sunlight and for conserving battery.
  • Menu System: Icon-based with options for white balance, exposure, and media selection.

6. Battery & Power

  • InfoLithium Batteries: Long-lasting, hot-swappable, shared with earlier models.
  • AC Adapter: For continuous use.

7. Flash & Output

  • Pop-up Flash: Automatic, with red-eye reduction.
  • Video Out: For TV/projector viewing.
  • Multi-Terminal: For accessories and data.

8. Additional Features

  • Self Timer: 10 seconds.
  • White Balance: Auto, Indoor, Outdoor, One Push for custom color.
  • Exposure Compensation: +/- 1.5 EV in 0.5-step increments.

User Experience

  • Image Quality: Strong for the era, especially for a camera with floppy capability.
  • Storage Flexibility: The ability to use both floppy and Memory Stick made it highly versatile.
  • Speed: Floppy write times slow; Memory Stick enabled burst shooting and video.
  • Portability: Still chunky due to lens and floppy drive, but smaller than high-zoom models.
  • Reliability: Well-built; floppy disks and memory sticks needed gentle handling.

Legacy & Impact

  • End of an Era: The FD100, along with the FD200, was the last true Sony Mavica to use floppy disks, representing the technological limits of the format.
  • Hybrid Pioneer: Helped users bridge from floppy disks to solid-state storage as the industry shifted.
  • Collector Value: Sought after for its rarity, hybrid storage, and as a historical capstone in digital photography.

Comparison to Other Mavica Models

  • FD200: Successor, similar body and features but with 3x zoom and higher max video capability.
  • FD95/FD97: 2.1MP, 10x zoom, larger bodies, more advanced; floppy and Memory Stick.
  • FD92: 2.1MP, 8x zoom, hybrid storage, earlier model.
  • FD87/FD85: 1.3MP, 6x/3x zoom, Memory Stick via adapter only.
  • FD73/FD88: VGA/1.2MP, lower resolution, more shots per floppy, floppy-only.

Trivia & Notes

  • The FD100 and FD200 are sometimes called the “last floppy Mavicas” and were produced in limited numbers.
  • MPEG-EX movie mode was only available when using a Memory Stick, not floppy.
  • The FD100’s hybrid design and 2MP sensor made it a practical choice for late adopters of digital imaging.

My Opinion:


The Sony Mavica FD100 represents both an evolution and a farewell within the iconic Mavica line. Building on the strengths of its predecessors, the FD100 offered a 2.1-megapixel sensor and 3x optical zoom, but its true innovation lay in its hybrid storage capability: not only could it use traditional 3.5" floppy disks, but it also featured a built-in slot for Sony’s Memory Stick, making it much more practical for users ready to embrace solid-state media. This flexibility was a game-changer at a time when floppy disks were rapidly becoming obsolete yet remained familiar and convenient for many users.

In terms of user experience, the FD100 was a camera designed for accessibility and ease-of-use. Its body was more compact and approachable than the bulkier “prosumer” FD95/FD97 models, making it ideal for families, travelers, and casual shooters. The camera’s controls, bright LCD, and reliable autofocus made everyday photography simple and enjoyable, while the increased storage capacity and speed of Memory Stick enabled longer shooting sessions, video capture, and faster image transfers.

What the FD100 excelled at was bridging old and new worlds. It allowed loyal Mavica fans to continue using floppies for quick sharing and archiving, while giving them a smooth path to the future of digital storage. For educators, small businesses, and anyone upgrading from early digital models, the FD100 was a practical, forward-thinking choice that didn’t force users to abandon their workflow overnight. Today, it stands as a transitional classic—valued for its flexibility, reliability, and for marking the end of an era in consumer digital photography.
I like to think of the FD100 as a battle class photography at its best.
