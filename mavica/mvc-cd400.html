Sony Mavica MVC-CD400: Full Details

Historical Context

  • Release Date: 2002
  • Lineage: The CD400 was the “pro” model of the CD Mavica series, featuring a larger sensor and premium Carl Zeiss lens.
  • Market Position: Advanced enthusiast to professional, for users demanding image quality and manual control.

Key Features & Specifications

Detailed Features

1. Sensor & Image Quality

  • 4MP for detailed prints up to 11x14".
  • JPEG and TIFF modes for compressed and lossless shooting.

2. Lens & Zoom

  • Premium Zeiss 3x zoom, f/2.0–2.5 lens for excellent low light and sharpness.
  • Macro mode.

3. Storage & Transfer

  • 156MB per CD, JPEG and TIFF storage.
  • USB for fast downloads.

4. Display & Controls

  • LCD and electronic viewfinder.
  • Full manual exposure, focus, WB, and flash.

5. Flash & Output

  • Pop-up flash, hot shoe for external flashes.

User Experience

  • Image Quality: Among the best of all Mavicas, rivaling early DSLRs.
  • Manual Controls: Ideal for creative work or demanding environments.
  • Storage: CD-RW support, but write speeds limited burst shooting.
  • Portability: Robust, “prosumer” build; slightly heavy, but manageable.

Legacy & Impact

  • Peak of CD Mavica: Set a high bar for image quality and features.
  • Collector Value: Highly desired by enthusiasts and retro shooters.

Comparison to Other Mavica Models

  • CD500: Successor, 5MP, similar features.
  • CD350: Mainstream, 3.2MP, fewer pro features.
  • CD1000: Older, 2.1MP, 10x zoom, bulkier.

Trivia & Notes

  • Best low-light performance of any CD Mavica.
  • Only CD Mavica with hot shoe for external flash.
  • TIFF mode for lossless imaging, a rarity in consumer cameras.

My Opinion:


The Sony Mavica CD400 stands as the ultimate and most technically ambitious entry in the Mavica CD series, representing both the zenith and conclusion of Sony’s unique approach to digital photography. Boasting a 4.0-megapixel CCD sensor—by far the highest in the Mavica lineup—the CD400 delivered image quality that could finally rival mainstream memory card cameras of its era. Its Carl Zeiss Vario-Sonnar 3x optical zoom lens provided exceptional sharpness and color fidelity, elevating the camera’s output to a level suitable for large prints, serious documentation, and even creative projects.

Where the CD400 truly excelled was in merging professional-grade photographic control with the signature convenience of direct-to-CD recording. Users could capture hundreds of high-resolution photos or extended MPEG videos and immediately burn them to mini CD-Rs, making sharing and archiving both simple and reliable. Manual controls for exposure, white balance, and focus, as well as advanced features like histogram display and hot shoe compatibility for external flash, gave enthusiasts and advanced amateurs tools rarely found in other consumer cameras of the time.

The CD400’s user experience was further enhanced by its responsive autofocus, bright LCD, ergonomic grip, and robust build quality—making it as practical in the field as it was in the studio. Its direct-to-disc workflow eliminated the need for cables or card readers and was especially valuable for educators, event photographers, and business users needing instant, portable backup and distribution of images.

In summary, the CD400 excelled as a bridge between the old and new in digital photography. It provided the convenience and archival reliability of optical media at the very moment memory cards were becoming dominant, and it delivered image quality and control that satisfied even demanding photographers. Today, the CD400 is celebrated by collectors and tech historians as the most advanced expression of the Mavica philosophy—a camera that pushed the limits of what direct-to-CD imaging could achieve, and a fitting finale to one of digital photography’s most innovative lineages. I truly admire the minds at Sony, they created a masterpiece in technololg. In a span of 20 years they changed photography period.
