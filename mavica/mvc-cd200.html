Sony Mavica MVC-CD200: Full Details

Historical Context

  • Release Date: 2002
  • Lineage: The CD200 was Sony’s second-generation CD Mavica, following the CD1000 but with a more compact body and simplified controls.
  • Market Position: Mid-range, aimed at mainstream consumers and families.

Key Features & Specifications

Feature Details
Image Sensor 2.0 MP, 1/2.7" CCD
Resolution 1600 x 1200 pixels
Lens 3x optical zoom, 6.3–19mm f/2.8–2.9 (39–117mm equiv.)
Digital Zoom 2x
Macro Macro mode, focus as close as ~10cm
Storage 8cm CD-R/CD-RW (156MB)
Image Format JPEG, TIFF, MPEG (movie mode)
Shots per CD Up to 160 (JPEG, depends on quality)
Movie Mode MPEG HQX, 320x240
LCD Display 2.5" TFT color LCD
Viewfinder None (LCD only)
Battery Sony NP-FM50 InfoLithium
AC Adapter Optional
Flash Built-in, pop-up
Self Timer Yes (10 seconds)
White Balance Auto, Indoor, Outdoor, One Push
Exposure Control +/- 1.5 EV, 0.5 step increments
Connectivity USB, A/V out, DC in
Dimensions Approx. 126 x 86 x 75 mm
Weight Approx. 410g (without battery/disc)
Software Sony PictureGear for Windows
Release Price ~$450 USD


Detailed Features

1. Sensor & Image Quality

  • 2MP CCD, good for 5x7" to 8x10" prints.
  • JPEG and TIFF options.

2. Lens & Zoom

  • 3x optical zoom (39–117mm equiv.), macro mode for close-ups.

3. Storage & Transfer

  • 156MB per CD—more than 100x capacity of a floppy.
  • Rewritable CD support.

4. Display & Controls

  • LCD-only operation, no viewfinder.
  • Simple, icon-driven menu.

5. Battery & Power

  • Uses standard InfoLithium battery.

6. Flash & Output

  • Built-in, pop-up flash; AV out.

User Experience

  • Image Quality: Good for the era, especially for casual and event photography.
  • Storage: CDs allow hundreds of shots per disc.
  • Speed: CD writing is slow, but great for archiving.
  • Portability: More compact than CD1000, easy to carry.
  • Reliability: CDs robust but must be kept clean.

Legacy & Impact

  • CD for Everyone: Helped make CD-based digital cameras accessible to the masses.
  • Collector Value: Sought after by collectors for its compact design and reliability.

Comparison to Other Mavica Models

  • CD1000: First-gen CD Mavica, larger, 10x zoom.
  • CD250: Nearly identical to CD200, minor body/firmware differences.
  • CD400/CD500: Higher-end, more advanced, Zeiss lens, higher resolution.

Trivia & Notes

  • The CD200 and CD250 are extremely similar—most differences are cosmetic or firmware-based.
  • CD-RW support meant you could erase and reuse discs.
  • No viewfinder—LCD only, which was a new trend for consumer cameras at the time.

My Opinion:


The Sony Mavica CD200 stands as one of the most advanced and forward-thinking cameras in the Mavica lineage, representing Sony’s final push to combine high-capacity optical storage with accessible digital imaging. Building on the innovation of the CD1000, the CD200 featured a 2.1-megapixel sensor and a versatile 3x optical zoom lens, delivering crisp, vibrant photos suitable for both everyday snapshots and serious documentation. Its most notable feature was the ability to record directly onto mini CD-R discs, vastly expanding storage capacity and allowing users to shoot hundreds of photos or lengthy MPEG videos without worrying about space or swapping media.

Where the CD200 truly excelled was in its user-friendly design and workflow efficiency. The camera’s CD-R storage meant instant archiving and simple sharing—users could hand out discs for immediate viewing on virtually any computer, a huge convenience in the days before widespread USB flash drives and cloud storage. The CD200’s streamlined controls, bright LCD, and improved menu system made it easy for beginners and advanced users alike. Its JPEG and MPEG video capabilities, coupled with reliable autofocus and solid build quality, made it an ideal choice for event photographers, educators, and business users who valued both capacity and simplicity.

In summary, the CD200 was a fitting finale for the Mavica family, blending the best of Sony’s optical media innovations with practical digital photography. It bridged the gap between the analog and digital worlds, and its legacy is remembered for making long-form, high-capacity digital shooting accessible at a time when such features were rare. Today, the CD200 is prized by collectors and tech enthusiasts for its unique place in the evolution of digital cameras, a testament to Sony’s creativity and commitment to user needs at the dawn of the 21st century.
