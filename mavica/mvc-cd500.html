Sony Mavica MVC-CD500: Full Details

Historical Context

  • Release Date: 2003
  • Lineage: The CD500 was the final and most advanced model of the CD Mavica line, pushing the format to its technical peak.
  • Market Position: Enthusiast/professional flagship for those demanding high resolution and advanced features.

Key Features & Specifications

Feature Details
Image Sensor 5.0 MP, 1/1.8" CCD
Resolution 2592 x 1944 pixels
Lens 3x Carl Zeiss Vario-Sonnar, 7–21mm f/2.0–2.5 (34–102mm equiv.)
Digital Zoom 2x
Macro Macro mode
Storage 8cm CD-R/CD-RW (156MB)
Image Format JPEG, TIFF, MPEG
Shots per CD ~100–200 (JPEG quality-dependent)
Movie Mode MPEG HQX, 320x240
LCD Display 1.8" TFT
Viewfinder Electronic (color EVF)
Battery Sony NP-FM50 InfoLithium
AC Adapter Optional
Flash Built-in, pop-up; hot shoe for external flash
Self Timer Yes (10 seconds)
White Balance Auto, Indoor, Outdoor, One Push
Exposure Control +/- 2.0 EV, 0.3 step increments
Connectivity USB, A/V out, DC in, hot shoe
Dimensions Approx. 139 x 97 x 101 mm
Weight Approx. 590g (without battery/disc)
Software Sony PictureGear for Windows
Release Price ~$1,000 USD




Detailed Features

1. Sensor & Image Quality

  • 5MP for crisp, detailed prints up to 13x19".
  • JPEG and TIFF for compressed and lossless images.

2. Lens & Zoom

  • Zeiss 3x zoom, fast f/2.0–2.5, sharp and bright.
  • Macro mode for close-ups.

3. Storage & Transfer

  • JPEG and TIFF to CD-R/CD-RW; up to 200 shots per disc.
  • USB connectivity.

4. Display & Controls

  • 1.8" LCD and high-res EVF.
  • Full manual and automatic modes.

5. Flash & Output

  • Pop-up flash and hot shoe for external flash.

User Experience

  • Image Quality: Top of the line for CD-based cameras, rivaled early compact DSLRs.
  • Manual Controls: Professional features in a compact body.
  • Storage: CDs allowed for easy archiving, but slow write time.
  • Portability: Solid, pro-style build.

Legacy & Impact

  • End of an Era: The highest-spec, last CD Mavica; closed out the format.
  • Collector Value: Highly prized by collectors and fans of retro digital tech.

Comparison to Other Mavica Models

  • CD400: Previous pro model, 4MP.
  • CD350: Mainstream, 3.2MP, fewer pro features.
  • CD1000: Much older, 2.1MP, 10x zoom, bulkier.

Trivia & Notes

  • Last CD Mavica produced; represents the technical peak of the CD camera era.
  • TIFF mode and Zeiss optics made it a cult favorite.
  • CD write speed, even at its best, was a limiting factor for action and burst shooting.

My Opinion:


The Sony Mavica CD500 stands as the final flourish in the storied Mavica lineage—a camera that not only embodied the best of the series, but also served as a meaningful coda to an era of innovation and accessibility in digital photography. With its 5-megapixel CCD sensor and a high-quality 3x Carl Zeiss Vario-Sonnar lens, the CD500 delivered image quality that could confidently compete with the best compact digital cameras of its time. Photos were rich in detail, color, and clarity, with enough resolution for serious prints and creative cropping, making it a true “prosumer” camera in a familiar and approachable package.

Where the CD500 truly excelled was in marrying advanced photographic controls with the unique direct-to-CD workflow that defined the Mavica brand. The ability to store hundreds of high-resolution images or extended MPEG videos on a single mini CD-R or CD-RW disc provided users with an archiving and sharing solution that was both simple and robust—no memory cards or proprietary formats required. Manual controls for exposure, focus, and white balance, along with features like TIFF capture and a hot shoe for external flash, gave enthusiasts and professionals the flexibility and creative freedom they demanded.

Perhaps the most poignant aspect of the CD500 is what it represents in the broader history of digital cameras. As the last Mavica, it closed the book on a series that had introduced millions to digital imaging, from the analog video floppies of the 1980s to the innovative hybrid and CD-based models of the 2000s. By the time of the CD500’s release, memory cards were rapidly becoming the industry standard, and the advantages of CD-based storage—so groundbreaking just a few years prior—were starting to fade. Yet, the CD500 remained relevant and beloved, especially among educators, archivists, and creative users who valued its unique workflow and robust reliability.

In conclusion, the Sony Mavica CD500 is more than just a camera—it’s a symbol of transition and ingenuity. It encapsulated the spirit of the Mavica series: making digital photography accessible, shareable, and fun. Its blend of high-end optics, advanced features, and convenient storage made it a fitting and dignified send-off for one of the most influential camera lines in history. Today, the CD500 is cherished by collectors and retro tech enthusiasts not just for its capabilities, but for its role as the graceful finale to Sony’s Mavica legacy.
